Neurodiversity is already in your talent pool.

Some players arrive with paperwork. Most do not, especially at ETC level, where players are drawn from grassroots clubs, schools, and representative teams with little shared history. The player most likely to be missed is not always the disruptive one. It is often the quiet, compliant, talented player whose struggles sit in education, communication, sensory load, emotional regulation, or feedback processing, exactly the player an identification programme is supposed to be built to catch.

Why this matters at ETC level

The interest that gets her signed can be the same trait that hides the diagnosis.

Girls can present differently, and one session a week with a wide intake gives you less time than anyone would like to catch what's going on.

01

ADHD doesn't always look hyperactive.

Most staff are trained to spot the disruptive presentation. A lot of girls with ADHD drift instead: polite, pleasant, permanently a little behind, starting things and not finishing them. Because she never causes a problem for anyone else, nobody investigates the problem she's having with herself.

02

An intense interest is a diagnostic marker, and also how she got here.

A restricted, intense interest is one of the things clinicians look for in autism. In a girl, that interest is more likely to be socially acceptable, and football is a common one. It doesn't trigger a referral. It gets her signed to your centre instead.

03

Masking is a cost, not a personality.

Autistic and ADHD girls can become highly skilled at copying, scripting, and performing "normal." A player who looks settled and coping in your session may be spending everything she has to look that way, then crashing where you can't see it.

04

The player who was "fine" at U12 can change fast around U14–15.

Puberty is when a lot of this becomes visible, or becomes a crisis. Girls are typically diagnosed with autism over a year later than boys on average, and often not until adolescence or adulthood. If a player who was coping well starts struggling and nothing external has obviously changed, that's worth asking about, not filing under "teenage hormones."

Parents chasing the Lionesses pathway tend to be well-informed on ADHD, autism and reasonable adjustments. And centres are monitored annually, with licensing decisions following those assessments. Under the Equality Act, adjustments that were never written down are extremely difficult to rely on.

What staff learn

The outcome is practical judgement.

The course gives staff enough understanding to stop over-personalising behaviour and start asking better questions. Not diagnosis. Better observation. Better adjustment. Better records.

The course now runs the whole journey, from first contact at intake through to a player leaving the programme, not just the period she's settled in and training well.

Recognise patterns.

Staff learn what neurodivergence can look like in the ETC environment, including the players who do not match the stereotype.

  • ADHD, autism, dyslexia, dyspraxia, dyscalculia, dysgraphia
  • Masking, shutdown, RSD, demand avoidance, sensory load
  • Developmental age versus chronological age
  • Body-signal differences: interoception, sleep, and fuelling, and why "I'm fine" isn't always reliable

Respond with fit.

Staff get concrete coaching, education, and communication strategies that do not require them to become clinicians.

  • How to change instruction, feedback, timing, and environment
  • How to support without lowering standards
  • How to work with parents without becoming defensive
  • Handling high-stakes moments: recruitment, the disciplinary conversation, and release

Record what matters.

The course lands in documentation because memory is not evidence. If an adjustment was tried, it needs a record.

  • Observation logs and player ND profiles
  • Adjustment records and review cycles
  • Cleaner handovers across age groups and departments

FAQ

Questions ETC leads usually ask first.

Is this live training or self-paced?

It is self-paced online training. Each staff member works through 12 modules at their own pace, with knowledge checks after every module, a final assessment, certificate generation, and downloadable resources on completion. There is no scheduled session, no cohort, and no waiting for a delivery date.

Who should complete it?

Centre and Talent ID leads, coaches, welfare and safeguarding leads, and trust, foundation or County FA managers overseeing the programme. The course is built so every role gets something directly applicable to their day-to-day, even where one person is covering more than one of those roles.

What happens if someone fails the final assessment?

Resits are available. The assessment has a 75% pass mark and is designed to be achievable by someone who has engaged with the course properly. Staff who do not pass on the first attempt can review the relevant modules and retake.

How does access work for an ETC Pack?

After purchase, your centre receives a registration link to forward to staff. Each staff member creates their own individual account, has their own login, and builds their own progress record and certificate. The purchasing admin gets a view of who has registered, how far they have got, and who has completed and been certified.

What if our centre needs more than 11 accounts?

Additional staff accounts can be added at £69 per person on top of the ETC Pack, at any time.

How long does access last?

The ETC Pack and any additional seats run for 12 months from the date of purchase.

Does it train staff to diagnose neurodivergence?

No. The course is explicit about staff not diagnosing. It trains staff to observe patterns, make practical adjustments, document what was tried, and refer or escalate appropriately when a concern needs specialist input.

Does this help with PGAAC monitoring?

Girls' ETCs are monitored annually by the Professional Game Academy Audit Company, via the Women's Talent Pathway Assessment Board, and licensing recommendations follow from those assessments. This course doesn't certify PGAAC compliance directly, but it gives you a documented, dated record of staff training on neurodiversity awareness and reasonable adjustments under the Equality Act 2010, the kind of evidence that supports your wider case file.

Does the course cover the performance and medical side, not just coaching and welfare?

Yes. A dedicated module covers how neurodivergence shows up in the body: interoception (why some players under-report pain, fatigue, and even concussion symptoms), sleep, fuelling and energy availability, and coordination. It's written for the whole staff, including anyone covering sports science or medical support, because the person who notices a player running on empty is usually the coach on the grass, not the doctor.

Does it cover what happens when a player leaves the programme?

Yes. Transitions out of a programme are hard, and they hit neurodivergent players especially hard. A dedicated module covers handling that conversation well, the mental-health risks involved, duty-of-care responsibilities, and how a player's support should travel with them if they move to another centre or club.

Is there a certificate, and can it be used as CPD evidence?

Yes. Passing the final assessment generates a certificate confirming the staff member has completed ND Training. Centres can retain certificates as evidence of staff CPD and internal training around neurodiversity awareness, reasonable adjustments, identification, and documentation practice.

Can we get an invoice for our finance team?

Yes. An invoice is generated automatically at purchase and can be forwarded to your club, trust, foundation or County FA finance team.

What is the course based on?

The evidence base includes UK legal sources, neurodevelopmental research, clinical guidance, and applied academy content. Key sources include the Equality Act 2010, NICE guidelines on ADHD (NG87) and autism (CG128), and UK GDPR guidance from the ICO. The evidence base also draws on JCQ Access Arrangements guidance for exams, the 2023 IOC consensus statement on Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport, autism sex-ratio research (Loomes, Hull & Mandy, 2017), research on late diagnosis in girls (Lockwood Estrin et al., 2021), and a duty-of-care framework for players leaving a programme.
