Know Your Brain

Your Fast Brain vs Your Thinking Brain

When something goes wrong — on the pitch or on the touchline — one part of your brain fires before the other one wakes up. Understanding which one just took over is the first step to actually controlling what happens next.

Who are you?
Two parts of your brain — both useful, not always at the right time
⚡
The Fast Brain
Reacts first
Fires instantly. It's there to protect you — but it doesn't stop to think. It's the part that reacts before the situation has even fully registered.
Emotional — angry, scared, frustrated
Fast — reacts before you can think
Protective — but often overreacts
Useful in actual danger. Less useful in football.
🧠
The Thinking Brain
Figures it out
Slower — but smarter. When it's in charge, you can make decisions, think about consequences, and actually choose your response instead of just reacting.
Logical — thinks before acting
Calm — can handle setbacks
Problem-solving — what can I do next?
The one you want in charge in tough moments
Scenarios — what does your brain do?

For each situation below, pick the response. There's no trick — just think honestly about which one you'd actually do first.

Situation 1 of 4
⚽
You step up for the penalty. You've been thinking about this moment all game. You blast it — straight at the keeper. What does your brain do first?
⚡ Fast Brain in charge
That reaction makes complete sense — your Fast Brain fired the moment the ball hit the keeper. It's not weakness, it's biology. The skill is noticing it happening and giving your Thinking Brain a chance to catch up. Three seconds is enough. What's the next thing you can control?
🧠 Thinking Brain in charge
That's hard to do. Your Fast Brain was screaming — and you still chose differently. That's not just good sportsmanship, it's one of the most useful skills you'll develop as a player. Your teammates notice it. Your coach notices it. And you'll forget about the penalty much faster.
Situation 2 of 4
🟨
The referee gives a free kick against you. You didn't even touch the other player — you know it. You actually know it. What does your brain do?
⚡ Fast Brain in charge
Every referee makes wrong calls. So do you, so does everyone. The decision is never going to change because you argued about it. But you could get booked — which is a decision that definitely won't go in your favour. The only thing arguing costs is your own focus.
🧠 Thinking Brain in charge
That takes real discipline — especially when you're convinced you're right. But the game has already moved on. By staying focused instead of arguing, you stayed in it. That's the mental edge that makes the difference between good players and great ones.
Situation 3 of 4
😔
Your teammate gives the ball away in the last minute. You're losing 2–1. It would have been 2–2. What do you do?
⚡ Fast Brain in charge
Your teammate already knows what happened — they felt it the moment they lost the ball. Anger or a glare doesn't teach them anything. What it does is knock their confidence exactly when you need them most. The best teams keep each other up, not down, when it goes wrong.
🧠 Thinking Brain in charge
That's what being a great teammate actually looks like. Not just the clapping and celebrating — the "don't worry" when it goes wrong. That kind of response builds trust in a team. Players play better when they know a mistake won't destroy the changing room atmosphere.
Situation 4 of 4
😤
An opposition player keeps fouling you. The referee keeps missing it. You've had enough. You can feel yourself getting angrier with every touch. What do you do?
⚡ Fast Brain in charge
Your Fast Brain is absolutely right that it's unfair. But retaliating means the referee sees you — not them. You get the card. They get away with it. That's not a trade worth making. The moment you feel the anger building is exactly when your Thinking Brain needs to step in. Notice it. Name it. Breathe.
✅ Smart play
That's the full toolkit working — the breath, the focus cue, and using the right channel to raise a concern. You stayed in the game, kept your head, and didn't give the opposition the reaction they were trying to get out of you. That's what composure actually looks like.
Scenarios — what does your brain do?

For each situation below, pick the response. These aren't obvious — think about what you'd actually do, not what you should do.

Situation 1 of 3
📣
It's matchday. A parent on your touchline has been calling out instructions that contradict yours all first half. You had a quiet word at half time. Ten minutes into the second half — they're doing it again. Fourteen other parents are watching.
⚡ Fast Brain in charge
That instinct to protect your authority in front of the group makes sense — you've already given them a chance. But calling it out publicly rarely lands the way you hope. Other parents witness a confrontation, not a resolution. The parent gets defensive, your players pick up on the tension, and you've now made it a public event. The private conversation after the game is harder to initiate — but it's the one that actually changes anything.
🧠 Thinking Brain in charge
That's genuinely difficult when you're feeling undermined in front of everyone. But the private conversation — especially when it's firm, not soft — is the one that sticks. You've kept the touchline calm for your players rather than escalating it, and you've given the parent no audience to perform for. That actually makes it easier to get the outcome you want.
Situation 2 of 3
👨‍👦
You're coaching a team that includes your own child. They lose the ball in a dangerous position late in the game and it leads directly to a goal. In the post-match debrief you find yourself addressing them directly — going into more detail than you would with any other player. It feels like you're holding them to a higher standard.
⚡ Fast Brain in charge
The Fast Brain is good at framing itself as the reasonable option. Whether that's "I'm holding them to a high standard" or "I'll handle it privately later" — both of those responses treat your child differently from everyone else in that room. Your child knows it. So do their teammates. Catching that mid-sentence and redirecting to the whole group is the harder call, but it's the one that keeps you in your role as their coach rather than their parent.
🧠 Thinking Brain in charge
Catching it mid-sentence is hard — most coaches don't notice until afterwards, if at all. Redirecting to the group rather than continuing to address your child directly is the move that protects them without letting the mistake go unaddressed. The group hears the coaching point. Your child doesn't carry the weight of having been singled out. And you walk out of that session as their coach, not their parent.
Situation 3 of 3
🤝
It's been a rough game. The opposition played on the edge all match and got away with a lot. Their manager spent 90 minutes making comments from their touchline — never directly at you, but loud enough. Final whistle. They walk over grinning with their hand out.
⚡ Fast Brain in charge
The temptation to say something — measured, not aggressive — feels like the composed response. It's not silence, it's not a scene. But the grin is bait, and anything pointed you say turns them into the victim and you into the problem. Ninety minutes of composure, handed back in the last ten seconds. Your players take their cue from you at the final whistle. The manager who walked away cleanly is always in a stronger position than the one who felt the need to make a point.
🧠 Thinking Brain in charge
Walking away cleanly after a game like that is harder than it looks. But your players will read that moment. If you're composed at the final whistle it signals that the result matters more than the argument — and next time you face that team, you've given nothing away. Sometimes the strongest thing you can do is say two words and turn back to your own people.

The gap is the skill

Between what happens and how you respond, there's a gap. It might only be a second. But that gap is where you actually have a choice. The more you practice choosing, the bigger that gap gets.
