Your Brain on Match Day

Why sometimes you react before you think

Everyone has two systems in their brain that compete for control. Understanding them is the first step to staying in control on the pitch.

THE CHIMP 🐒
Reacts FAST 5x faster than your thinking brain
Emotional Runs on feelings, not facts
Fight or flight Sees everything as a threat
No consequences Doesn't think about what happens next
Loud Shouts over your thinking brain
THE HUMAN 🧠
Thinks first Considers what's actually happening
Rational Uses facts, not just feelings
Sees the big picture Thinks about the whole game
Knows consequences Understands what a red card means
Calm Makes better decisions under pressure
Match Day Example

Someone shouts something nasty about your teammate. Your Chimp wants to shout back or push them. Your Human knows that if you react, YOU get the card, YOUR team plays with 10, and YOU miss next week. The person who shouted? Nothing happens to them. The Chimp makes it worse. The Human protects you.

The Key Thing to Remember
Your Chimp is faster. But your Human is smarter. Train your Human to catch up.

The 10-Second Rule

How to stop your Chimp from taking over

When something makes you angry, your brain floods with chemicals that last about 90 seconds. You don't need to count to 90. You just need to survive the first 10.

1
STOP
Don't move. Don't speak. Just stop.

Your Chimp wants you to react RIGHT NOW. Every second you wait is a second your Human brain is catching up.

2
BREATHE
One deep breath in through your nose, out through your mouth.

This isn't soft — it's science. Deep breathing slows the chemicals flooding your brain. Elite athletes do it before every penalty.

3
LOOK
Look at something that isn't the problem.

Breaking eye contact stops your Chimp from locking on. It gives your Human space to think.

4
CHOOSE
Now decide. What's the SMART thing to do?

After 10 seconds, your Human has caught up. You can still be angry — but now you choose what to do instead of just reacting.

You Can't Control What Happens.
But you can control what you do next. Ten seconds is all it takes.

What to Say When It Kicks Off

Words that help — and words that make it worse

When someone around you is losing it — a teammate, an opponent, a parent — what you say matters. Some words pour water on the fire. Others pour petrol.

✗ Don't Say This
✓ Say This Instead
"Calm down!"
Their Chimp hears this as a challenge. Makes it worse every time.
"Yeah, that was well out of order"
You're on their side without making things bigger. Their Chimp feels heard.
"What's your problem?"
Sounds aggressive even if you don't mean it. Their Chimp hears a fight.
"Come on, let's go get some water"
Moving someone away from the situation gives their brain a chance to reset.
"You're being ridiculous"
Judging someone when they're emotional shuts them down completely.
"Leave it, they're not worth it"
Gives them a way out that doesn't feel like backing down. Much easier to take.
"It's only a game"
To them right now it's NOT just a game. This dismisses their feelings.
"That was so frustrating, I get it"
Showing you understand what they're feeling helps them calm down faster.
"Whatever"
Walking away with attitude feels like winning. It actually escalates things.
"You alright? What happened?"
Letting them tell you releases the pressure. You don't have to fix anything.
You don't have to fix it. You don't have to win it.
Sometimes the best thing you can do is just not make it worse.

It's Not Always What You Think

Why people sometimes react in ways you don't expect

You know that teammate who always seems to overreact? Or the kid on the other team who can't stop arguing with the ref? Before you judge them — tap each card to see what might really be going on.

What you see

A player kicks off after a foul and gets a yellow card. After the game, they're really upset and sorry.

Tap to see what might be happening ↓
What might be happening

They might be neurodivergent — meaning their brain is wired differently. Some people's brains react more intensely and they genuinely can't always control it. The remorse afterwards is real.

What you see

A parent starts shouting at the ref over a throw-in. It seems totally over the top.

Tap to see what might be happening ↓
What might be happening

You don't know what's happening in their life. They might be dealing with problems at work, at home, or with their health. The throw-in isn't really what they're angry about.

What you see

Someone on the other team says something offensive. Their teammates look embarrassed.

Tap to see what might be happening ↓
What might be happening

Sometimes people repeat things they've heard at home or online without fully understanding how it lands. That doesn't make it okay — but understanding why helps you respond better.

What YOU can do

Don't laugh at someone who's lost control. They probably feel worse about it than you do.
Check in with a teammate after the game. A simple "you alright?" is enough — you don't need to say anything else.
If someone says something offensive, you don't have to confront them yourself. Tell your coach. That's not grassing — that's the right call.
Being kind costs nothing. Being unkind can cost everything.
You Never Know the Full Story.
How you treat people when they're at their worst says everything about you.

Your Club, Your Rules

What a great matchday actually looks like

Everyone talks about bad behaviour in football. But most matchdays are brilliant. Here's what the best clubs and the best players do — and how you can be part of it.

Before the Game
Shake hands like you mean it

Not because someone told you to. Because you're about to share a pitch with these people for 90 minutes.

Leave your baggage at the gate

Bad day at school? Argument at home? That's real — but it doesn't belong on the pitch. Park it for now.

Agree with your mates

"Let's not give them anything to complain about today." Set the standard before kickoff.

During the Game
Clap the other team's good play

Seriously. It changes the whole atmosphere. And it confuses the opposition — which is a bonus.

Talk to the ref, don't shout at them

"Ref, I thought that was a foul" works. "REF, ARE YOU BLIND?" doesn't. Same message, very different result.

Pick people up

Opponent down injured? Help them up. Teammate made an error? Encourage them. That's leadership.

After the Game
Win or lose, be the same person

Nobody respects a bad winner. Losing with dignity takes more guts than winning ever does.

Thank the ref

Even if they had a shocker. They turned up so you could play. Most weekends, not enough of them do.

Check in on someone

If someone had a tough game, a simple "you alright?" makes more difference than you think.

Football is the best thing going.
The people who make it brilliant aren't the ones who shout the loudest. They're the ones who set the standard. That can be you.
