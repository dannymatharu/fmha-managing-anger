Code of Conduct

Parents, Carers & Guardians / Spectators

Grassroots football exists for the players — children who show up every week to learn, compete, and enjoy the game. How adults behave on the sideline shapes that experience directly. This Code sets out what we expect from every parent, carer, guardian, and spectator at every game.

🏟️
Your Club Name FC
Please sign and return this form to your Club Welfare Officer
My commitments as a parent, carer, guardian or spectator
1
Cheer for the players — not against the opposition
Positive encouragement is always welcome. Negative comments directed at opposing players, their parents, carers, or guardians, or their coaches are not. Shout their name. Celebrate effort as much as goals.
2
You are responsible for anyone you bring
If a family member, partner, or friend attends a match with you, their behaviour is your responsibility. This Code applies to them just as it applies to you. If someone you brought is asked to leave or causes an incident, you will be held accountable alongside them.
3
Stay behind the touchline and within the designated spectator area
Where a designated spectators' area is marked, stay within it. Never enter the field of play. These boundaries protect players, officials, and the game — and they apply to every match, home and away.
4
Respect the referee — full stop
Referees — including youth referees — make mistakes. So does every player on the pitch. Shouting at officials, disputing decisions, or using abusive language towards them is unacceptable. If you disagree with a decision, say nothing during the game and raise it through official channels afterwards.
5
Know your triggers — and pause before you act on them
Watching your child miss a penalty or get substituted is emotionally hard. That reaction is human. But it is not a reason to shout, argue, or embarrass yourself or your child. Notice the feeling, take a breath, and choose your response.
6
Leave coaching to the coach
Instructions shouted from the sideline — especially contradicting what the coach has said — confuse players and undermine the coaching relationship. Trust the coach. Your job on match day is to be a supportive presence, not a second manager.
7
When players make mistakes, offer encouragement
Mistakes are part of learning. Whether it's your child or another team's player, a missed shot or a poor pass, the response that helps is "keep going" — not criticism. Children remember how they were made to feel far longer than the result.
8
Zero tolerance on discrimination
Any conduct referencing a player's or official's ethnicity, race, nationality, religion, gender, sexual orientation, disability, or neurodivergence — whether as a comment, gesture, or chant — will be reported to the club and may be referred to the FA. Under FA Rule E21 this may result in police action.
9
Raise concerns the right way
If you have concerns about player welfare, coaching decisions, or another spectator's behaviour, speak to the Club Welfare Officer — not to the individual involved during or immediately after a match. You are also responsible for making yourself familiar with the club's safeguarding practices.
10
Recognise that every player experiences the game differently
Some players in your child's team may be neurodivergent — living with ADHD, autism, dyslexia, or another condition that affects how they process, communicate, and respond under pressure. In most cases you won't know, and you don't need to. What you do need to do is avoid assumptions about a player's attitude, effort, or reactions. A child who seems to struggle with instructions, reacts differently to setbacks, or finds the matchday environment overwhelming may be dealing with something invisible. Patience, inclusion, and a supportive sideline make a real difference.
11
Remember what this is for
Only 15% of young people felt safe at their football match (Suffolk FA / Active Suffolk survey). Your behaviour is either part of that problem or part of the solution. The children are watching — including yours.
⚠️ Club accountability — what you may not know

Under FA Rules E20 and E21, your club is held responsible for the conduct of its supporters at both home and away fixtures. Spectator misconduct is not just a personal matter — it counts against the entire club.

A team accumulating 50 or more penalty points in a season triggers a club charge. Four or more incidents of violent conduct across any of the club's teams within 12 months results in a formal charge. Sanctions include mandatory fines, league points deductions, and removal of England Football Accreditation. In 2023–24, 20 teams had their affiliation removed or placed under an action plan.

If I do not follow this Code, I understand I may be
→Issued with a verbal warning or asked to leave the match
→Required to meet with the club committee, league, or County FA Welfare Officer
→Obliged to undertake an FA education course
→Requested not to attend future games, or suspended from the ground
→Required to leave the club — along with any dependents — and/or issued a fine
→Reported to the FA or County FA, with potential police involvement for serious offences

Code of Conduct

Junior Players — U12 to U16

Football teaches you more than how to play. How you handle a bad decision, a missed chance, or a loss tells you — and everyone watching — who you are. This Code sets out what we expect from you on and off the pitch.

⚽
Your Club Name FC
Sign and return to your coach or Club Welfare Officer
When playing football, I will
1
Always play my best — for the benefit of the team
Not just when I feel like it. Effort is the one thing every player controls, regardless of ability, the scoreline, or how the game is going.
2
Respect the referee, even when I disagree
Referees miss calls — just like I miss passes. Shouting at them, arguing, or showing dissent through gestures could get me booked and it makes me look worse than the decision did. Say nothing in the moment; talk to my coach afterwards.
3
Understand that my coach makes decisions for the whole team
Substitutions, game time, and tactics are decisions my coach makes based on what's best for the team overall — not just me. I will trust that process, and if I'm unhappy, I will talk about it calmly after the game rather than on the pitch.
4
Control my reactions — breathe before I act
When something goes wrong, my body fires up before my brain catches up. The gap between the trigger and my response is where I actually have a choice. Three deep breaths. That's enough time to decide not to do the thing I'll regret.
5
Encourage my teammates — especially when it's hard
A teammate who just made a mistake already knows. Shouting at them or blaming them makes things worse. A good teammate says "don't worry, keep going" — that takes more character than celebrating when things go well.
6
No abusive language — towards anyone
This includes opponents, referees, my own teammates, and anyone watching. Language that targets someone's background, appearance, or identity is not banter — it's abuse. If I'm unsure, I ask: would I say it to their face in front of my coach?
7
What I post online represents my club
Social media is not separate from the game. Posting negative comments about opponents, referees, or teammates — or sharing videos or images intended to embarrass someone — is a breach of this Code. If I wouldn't say it in front of my coach, I shouldn't post it. Online conduct can result in the same consequences as conduct on the pitch.
8
Win and lose with the same attitude
Shake hands at the end of every game — win or lose — with the other team, the officials, and the managers. The players who develop the furthest are the ones who stay level whatever the scoreline.
9
Respect facilities home and away
Treat changing rooms, pitches, and club facilities with respect wherever I play. How I treat the environment tells people something about my character.
10
Speak to someone if something's not right
If something is bothering me — on the pitch, in the changing room, or online — I will tell my coach or Club Welfare Officer. Staying silent when something isn't right doesn't make me tough. Speaking up takes more confidence than saying nothing.
🛡️ Safeguarding

Every player at this club has the right to feel safe. If you are concerned about your own welfare or the welfare of another player, speak to the Club Welfare Officer — their details are available from the club secretary. You can also find guidance at EnglandFootball.com or contact the FMHA via thefmha.com.

If I do not follow this Code, I understand I may be
→Asked to apologise to whoever I have upset
→Given a formal warning by my coach or club
→Dropped, substituted, or suspended from training
→Required to meet with the Club Welfare Officer or club committee

Code of Conduct

Junior Players — U7 to U11

Football is better when everyone plays with kindness, effort, and respect. Here are six things we ask every player to promise — not just because your coach said so, but because you actually mean it.

🌟
Your Club Name FC
Sign and bring back to your coach
My six promises
🤝
I will be kind to everyone
To my teammates, to the other team, and to the referee — even when things go wrong.
👂
I will listen to my coach and the referee
They are here to help me get better and keep the game fair for everyone.
💪
I will try my best — every time
Not just when I feel like it. Effort is the one thing I always control.
😤
I will stay calm when things go wrong
Missing a shot or making a mistake doesn't make me a bad player. Getting angry about it doesn't help anyone.
🙌
I will encourage my teammates
If someone makes a mistake, I'll say "don't worry" — not shout at them. Good teammates lift each other up.
🏆
I will say "well played" to the other team
Whether we win or lose. That's what being a good sport actually means.
🛡️ If something worries you

If anything makes you feel unsafe or unhappy at football — on the pitch, in the changing room, or anywhere else — tell your coach, a parent, carer, or guardian, or the Club Welfare Officer straight away. You can always talk to a trusted adult. You won't get in trouble for speaking up.

If I don't keep my promises, I understand I may be
→Asked to say sorry to whoever I upset
→Given a warning by my coach
→Left out of training or a match

Code of Conduct

Coaches & Managers

Coaches and managers set the tone for everything. The standards you model — how you treat officials, how you respond to setbacks, how you talk about opponents — become the standards your players adopt. This Code sets out what the club and the FA expect from every person in a coaching or management role.

📋
Your Club Name FC
Please sign and return to the Club Welfare Officer
As a coach or manager, I will
1
Put player wellbeing above everything else
The safety and welfare of every player in my care comes before results, league position, or personal reputation. I will not place pressure on players — or allow others to — in ways that compromise their physical or mental health. Development of the whole person matters more than development of performance.
2
Respect every player's dignity — without exception
I will treat every player fairly and with respect, regardless of ability, age, background, or personality. I will not favour, belittle, or exclude. I will not use foul, racist, discriminatory, or abusive language at any time — in training, on the touchline, or in the changing room.
3
Recognise and support neurodivergent players
Some players may be neurodivergent — living with ADHD, autism, dyslexia, or another condition that shapes how they learn, communicate, and handle pressure. I will not mistake these differences for attitude problems or lack of effort. I will adapt my coaching where possible and ensure my sessions and environment are as inclusive as I can make them. If I need guidance, I will speak to the Club Welfare Officer or seek appropriate support.
4
Make expectations clear — for both players and myself
From the start of the season I will make clear to players what I expect of them and what they can expect of me. Clear, consistent expectations reduce anxiety, build trust, and create the kind of environment where players can focus on playing.
5
Respect match officials — always
I will not argue with, abuse, or intimidate referees or other officials. I will not encourage players or spectators to do so either. If I believe a decision was incorrect, I will raise it through official channels after the match — not on the touchline during it.
6
Always promote fair play and the spirit of the game
I will never condone cheating, time-wasting, gamesmanship, or violations of the Laws of the Game. I will not permit or ignore the use of prohibited substances or techniques. I will make fair play a consistent, visible part of how my team trains and competes.
7
Help players take responsibility for their own behaviour
My job is not just to coach football — it is to help players develop as people. I will guide them towards taking ownership of their conduct, their effort, and their reactions. Players who understand why behaviour matters will carry those standards beyond the pitch.
8
Report discipline issues to the club immediately
Any incident involving misconduct — before, during, or after a match — will be reported to the club without delay. I will not attempt to address issues directly with the FA or league independently; all formal communications on behalf of the team go through the club secretary. This protects players, protects me, and protects the club.
9
Ensure all registrations and safeguarding are up to date
I am responsible for ensuring that all team officials — including helpers and DBS applicants — are registered on the FA Club Portal and that all safeguarding qualifications are valid for the current season. I will not allow anyone to operate in an unregistered capacity.
10
Maintain high standards of conduct and appearance at all times
Players take their cue from the adults around them. My body language, my language, and how I carry myself in and around the game are all being observed and absorbed. I will hold myself to the same standards I ask of my players.
⚠️ Fines & discipline

Fines issued by the FA to individual players must be paid promptly by the player. Fines issued by leagues for administrative matters are the responsibility of the manager of the relevant team and must be paid without delay. Any fines left unpaid will result in the player or official in question being suspended from playing and training until the matter is resolved.

If I do not follow this Code, I understand I may be
→Given a formal warning by the club committee
→Required to undertake an FA education or safeguarding course
→Suspended from the touchline or from training duties
→Removed from my role at the club
→Reported to the FA or County FA — with potential police involvement for serious offences
