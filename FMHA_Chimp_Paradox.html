The Chimp Paradox

A simple but powerful model for understanding and managing our emotions — helping players, coaches, and parents respond more effectively under pressure.

Your brain operates using three parts
🐒
The Chimp
Emotional & Instinctive
Reacts impulsively with emotion and instinct
Responsible for anger, frustration, and emotional outbursts
Often acts before thinking — it fires 4× faster than rational thought
🧠
The Human
Logical & Rational
Thinks things through calmly and makes rational decisions
Helps regulate emotions and make good choices
Slower to engage — needs 5–10 seconds to take over from the Chimp
💻
The Computer
Learned Responses & Habits
Stores past experiences, habits, and learned behaviours
Can be programmed to respond automatically in helpful ways
The goal: train it so the right response becomes automatic
By understanding these three parts, we can recognise emotional responses, regulate them, and make better decisions on and off the pitch. The key insight: emotional reactions are natural — but you can manage them. You are not your Chimp.
Recognising your Chimp in action
Feeling angry after a bad referee decision
Blaming teammates when things go wrong
Reacting aggressively to a foul
Strategies to stay in control
✓
Pause and Breathe
Take a deep breath before reacting. The Human brain needs those few seconds to engage.
✓
Use a Reset Phrase
Say something like "Next play" or "Stay composed" — a pre-set phrase interrupts the Chimp and redirects focus.
✓
Walk Away
Step away for a moment if needed. Physical distance from the trigger breaks the feedback loop.
✓
Channel Frustration Positively
Use the energy to improve your performance rather than argue. The Chimp's energy isn't bad — it's misdirected.
✓
Train Your Computer
Practise emotional control in training to develop automatic habits. The more you practise the response, the more automatic it becomes.
Example
A referee makes a questionable call. Your Chimp wants to argue, but your Human reminds you that complaining won't change the decision. You refocus on the game and move forward.
Recognising your Chimp in action
Feeling frustrated when players don't follow instructions
Yelling at referees or opponents
Showing visible anger when the team is losing
Strategies to stay in control
✓
Model Calmness
Players take cues directly from you. Staying composed helps them stay focused — your emotional state is contagious.
✓
Redirect Emotional Energy
Instead of shouting, give constructive feedback. The same energy that fuels frustration can fuel better coaching.
✓
Use Rational Thinking
Remind yourself of the bigger picture — player development over match results. Ask: "What does this player need from me right now?"
✓
Teach Players to Manage Their Chimps
Incorporate emotional regulation drills in training. Name the model. When players understand it, they can use it.
Example
A player reacts angrily after being substituted. Your Chimp wants to snap back, but your Human calmly explains the tactical reason and reassures the player — turning a flashpoint into a coaching moment.
Recognising your Chimp in action
Shouting at referees after a bad call
Criticising your child loudly from the sideline
Comparing your child's performance to others
Strategies to stay in control
✓
Focus on Encouragement
Cheer effort, not just results. Your child performs better when they feel supported, not evaluated.
✓
Pause Before Reacting
Take a deep breath before speaking. The 5 seconds it takes to breathe is the gap between your Chimp and your Human.
✓
Avoid Referee Arguments
Set an example of respect and sportsmanship. Your child is watching — and so is every other child on that touchline.
✓
Reflect on Your Role
Ask yourself: "Am I helping or making things worse?" Honest self-assessment is the Human brain doing its job.
Example
Your child misses a penalty. Your Chimp wants to shout advice, but your Human remembers that mistakes are learning opportunities. You instead say, "Great effort! Keep going."
Five things to take away
1
You Are Not Your Chimp
Emotional reactions are natural, but you can manage them. The Chimp is part of you — it's not all of you.
2
Recognise Your Triggers
Understand when your Chimp is taking over. Awareness is the first step — you can't manage what you can't see.
3
Pause & Engage the Human Brain
Take a moment before reacting impulsively. Those 5–10 seconds are everything — they're the gap where the Human can intervene.
4
Train the Computer
Develop automatic responses to emotional situations through deliberate practice. The right habits become the default response.
5
Football Is Emotional — and That's OK
Emotional intelligence leads to better performance, better relationships, and more enjoyment. Managing the Chimp isn't about being less passionate — it's about being more effective.
Final thought

By managing our Chimps, we create a more positive and respectful football environment where players develop resilience, coaches lead with composure, and parents support without pressure.
