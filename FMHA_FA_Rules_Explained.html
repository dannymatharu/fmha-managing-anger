FA Rules — Plain English

Your Club Is Responsible. Here's What That Actually Means.

Most club officials know spectator behaviour is "bad for the club." Very few know that the FA has a specific, structured framework that can result in fines, points deductions, and the removal of your club's affiliation. This is that framework, in plain English.

The two rules that govern your club's liability
FA Rule E20
Club responsibility for players and officials
Your club is responsible for the conduct of its directors, players, coaches, officials, employees, and any representatives attending a match. When they don't behave properly, the charge comes to the club — not just the individual. Note: spectator and supporter behaviour is a separate liability governed by Rule E21.
FA Rule E21
Club responsibility for spectators
Your club is responsible for the behaviour of its supporters at home and away fixtures. If a parent or follower of your club behaves badly — anywhere, at any of your teams' games — your club is answerable for it.
20
Teams had affiliation removed or action-planned in 2024–25 In the 2024–25 season alone, 20 teams had their FA affiliation immediately removed or placed under a formal action plan due to accumulated conduct issues. This is not a theoretical risk.
🏟️ Home AND away — yes, both

A common misconception: many clubs assume they're only liable for behaviour at their home ground. Under Rule E21, you are responsible for your supporters at every fixture your teams play — home or away.

If one of your parents abuses a referee or an opposition player at an away ground, your club will be charged. The location is irrelevant. The connection to your club is what matters.

What happens to your club if found responsible
💷
Fines
For individuals, the mandatory minimum fine is £75. For club misconduct charges involving a protected characteristic (e.g. discrimination), the recommended entry point is £100–£200 depending on the level of football. Fines scale with severity and prior history.
📉
League points deductions
For repeated serious misconduct — including spectator abuse — the FA can deduct league points from any or all of your teams.
🏅
Loss of accreditation
Accumulated penalty points can result in removal of your England Football Accreditation — affecting funding, facilities access, and reputation.
🚫
Removal from competition
A specific team can be removed from their league mid-season. In the most serious cases, a team's FA affiliation can be suspended or cancelled — England Football's own headline example in 2025 was the first-ever team affiliation removal under the toughened sanctions regime.
Can your club defend itself?

Yes — but the bar is high, and the defence does not apply to every charge. For charges under E21.1–E21.3 (improper conduct, missile throwing, and pitch encroachment), your club can raise a defence if it can prove two things: that the incident was completely outside its control, and that its officials used "all due diligence" to prevent it.

This defence does not apply in the same way to E21.4 — discriminatory supporter conduct. If a supporter engages in discriminatory behaviour, the due-diligence route is not available to the club in the same way. The threshold for what the club must demonstrate is materially higher, and the charge framework is different. Do not assume "all due diligence" covers every type of spectator misconduct charge.

For E21.1–E21.3 charges, the Regulatory Commission will consider the severity of the incident, the steps the club took beforehand, and whether there is a history of similar issues. Having a documented Code of Conduct, trained welfare officers, and a pre-match briefing process all count as evidence of due diligence. Not having them makes any defence significantly harder.

In short: the clubs that escape charges are the ones who can show they were actively trying to prevent the problem — not the ones who say they didn't know it was their responsibility.

What your club should have in place — now

📄
A signed Code of Conduct for every parent and spectator Collected at registration — not just posted on a noticeboard. Signature matters if you ever need to evidence due diligence.
🎓
Trained welfare officers who understand their role on match days The CWO's job isn't just DBS checks and admin. They need to know how to recognise and de-escalate sideline tension before it becomes a formal incident.
📋
A documented incident log Every behavioural incident — however minor — should be logged with time, date, witnesses, and outcome. This is your evidence trail if charges are brought.
📢
A pre-match briefing process for high-risk fixtures Designating a sideline monitor, welcoming both sets of supporters before kick-off, and reminding everyone of the Code of Conduct — all demonstrable steps.
🔄
Regular committee agenda items on behaviour and culture FMHA training data consistently shows voluntary attendance at behaviour workshops runs at roughly 15–20% of eligible club staff. Making behaviour and welfare a standing agenda item — not a one-off workshop — is the difference between clubs that improve and those that wait for a charge.
