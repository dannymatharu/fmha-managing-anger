Why It Matters

What unchecked anger
costs the game

Referees
Referee Abuse
Verbal abuse towards officials is endemic in grassroots football. It normalises aggression, affects the wellbeing of young referees, and drives officials out of the game.
Players
Players Dropping Out
Children cite sideline negativity as a top reason for leaving sport. Anxiety around matchdays recorded from age 9.
Clubs
Clubs Losing Standing
Repeated misconduct leads to FA fines, points deductions, and loss of Charter Standard — affecting funding and facilities.
The Key Insight
5,000
emotional reactions per day — most before you've consciously thought about them
The gap between trigger and response is where everything happens. Most people believe their anger is caused by the event — the bad tackle, the referee's call. It isn't. It's caused by the story they tell themselves in the milliseconds that follow. That story is changeable. That is the whole point of this training.
The Science

Your Chimp Brain

Two systems. One very fast. One much more useful.
THE CHIMP
Limbic system / emotional brain
  • Fires 4× faster than rational thought
  • Cannot tell physical danger from social embarrassment
  • Hijacks body — heart rate, voice, posture
  • Thinks in black and white
  • Once active, it's running the show
THE HUMAN
Prefrontal cortex / thinking brain
  • Slower — takes 5–10 seconds to engage
  • Capable of perspective, reason, empathy
  • Can override the Chimp — if given the chance
  • Responds to deliberate pause and slow breathing
  • The goal: buy it those 5–10 seconds
Based on Prof. Steve Peters' Chimp Paradox model (2012)
The Model

The Escalation Ladder

Five levels. It climbs fast — and the earlier you catch it, the easier it is to manage.
5
PHYSICAL
Assault, throwing objects, uncontrollable rage
4
THREATENING
Direct threats, squaring up, intimidation
3
VERBAL
Shouting, swearing, personal insults, discriminatory language
2
AGITATED
Muttering, pacing, exaggerated reactions, tense body
1
FRUSTRATED
Sighing, eye-rolling, quiet complaints to the person next to them
Intervention is most effective at levels 1–2. By level 4, physical risk is real.
Important Distinction

When it's not anger —
it's overload

ANGER
  • Intentional — the person is choosing their behaviour
  • Escalates with attention or challenge
  • Calms when removed from the trigger
  • Linked to a specific perceived injustice
  • Responds to boundary-setting and consequence
OVERLOAD / OVERWHELM
  • Unintentional — the person has lost capacity to regulate
  • Escalates with instructions or demands
  • Needs a calm, low-stimulation space to recover
  • Often linked to sensory processing or anxiety (incl. neurodivergence)
  • Responds to patience, simplicity, and physical space
If unsure which you're looking at — default to the Overload response. It is never harmful.

Techniques 01 & 02

Pause, Breathe & Name It

01
Pause & Breathe
Why it works
Slow diaphragmatic breathing activates the parasympathetic nervous system, counteracting the Chimp's adrenaline surge. A single 5-second exhale starts reducing heart rate and cortisol. You don't need to feel calm — you just need to breathe slowly.

5 seconds in · 5 seconds out · repeat ×3
02
Acknowledge the Emotion
Why it works
Labelling an emotion ("I'm feeling angry right now") activates the prefrontal cortex and dampens the Chimp's response. This is not suppression — it's acknowledgement.

"I'm frustrated right now."
"That's anger. Not danger."
Lieberman et al., 2007 — "Putting Feelings into Words", Psychological Science
Techniques 03 & 04

Reset & Anchor

03
Trigger Question
Why it works
"Is what I'm about to do going to help or harm the young person in front of me?" Any pre-set question that redirects focus from the trigger to the purpose. Ask it before you speak. Every time.
04
Rest Phrase
Why it works
A single word or short phrase — your mental anchor. Interrupts rumination. Pre-choose it in calm moments.

"Just coach."
"My kids."
"This moment."
Techniques 05 & 06

Step Back & Let Go

05
Step Outside
Why it works
Physically leave — even 10 metres — when activation is high. Removes the sensory triggers feeding the Chimp. Breaks the feedback loop that crowd noise and visuals sustain.

Move first. Think second.
06
Create Distance
Why it works
Emotionally detach from the result. Become the observer. Most touchline anger is outcome-linked — release investment in the result and you remove the Chimp's fuel.

"The result is not mine to control."
Know Who You're Dealing With — Type 1 of 5

The Disinterested Parent/Carer/Guardian

1
Disinterested Parent/Carer/Guardian
What drives them
Attendance feels obligatory. Not invested in football — they're counting down, checking their phone. Boredom turns into critical commentary when they've got nothing else to do.
What it looks like
  • Eye-rolling, sarcasm, side conversations
  • Sporadic outbursts — then goes back to their phone
  • Critical of decisions without real investment
How to respond
  • Give them a role before they disengage: "Can you watch the left flank and give me a read at half-time?"
  • Purpose changes behaviour faster than any confrontation
  • Don't challenge — recruit
Know Who You're Dealing With — Type 2 of 5

The Injustice Seeker

2
Injustice Seeker
What drives them
A deep need for fairness to be acknowledged — even when their reading of the situation is wrong. The call is secondary. What they need is to feel heard.
What it looks like
  • Fixates on one decision and repeats it
  • Gets louder when challenged or dismissed
  • Arguments with officials, coaches, even other parents/carers/guardians
How to respond
  • Never argue the call — you won't win, and trying escalates them
  • "I know that felt wrong — let's not let it cost us"
  • Acknowledge the frustration without agreeing. Then redirect to what they can control.
Know Who You're Dealing With — Type 3 of 5

The Status Protector

3
Status Protector
What drives them
Their identity is entangled with their child's performance. A poor game is a public failure for them personally. The pitch has become a stage for their self-worth.
What it looks like
  • Directed criticism at their own child from the touchline
  • Comparing their child to others; undermining the coach
  • Performing outrage for other parents/carers/guardians watching
How to respond
  • Remove the audience — move them away from the group first
  • "They're having a tough one — they need your voice, not the result"
  • Separate their identity from the child's performance, gently and privately
Know Who You're Dealing With — Type 4 of 5

The Contagion Carrier

4
Contagion Carrier
What drives them
Social contagion — they're feeding off and amplifying the group's energy. Often not the initial trigger. They didn't start the fire but they're the ones keeping it burning.
What it looks like
  • Validates whoever is loudest with "exactly" and "that's right"
  • Escalation is social, not personal — they're performing for the group
  • Often visibly surprised when challenged directly
How to respond
  • Physical separation is the most reliable tool — break the feedback loop
  • A quiet word away from the group often works; without an audience, the behaviour loses its fuel
  • "Come and stand with me for a bit" — calm and non-confrontational
Know Who You're Dealing With — Type 5 of 5

The Digital Chimp

5
Digital Chimp
What drives them
Online disinhibition — keyboard distance removes social consequence. Behaviour crosses lines that would never happen face-to-face. The Chimp runs unchecked because there's no physical social feedback to stop it.
What it looks like
  • Post-match WhatsApp complaints that become pile-ons
  • Public social media criticism of players, referees, or club officials
  • Behaviour escalates as others join in online
How to respond
  • Screenshot before you do anything else
  • Address privately, one-to-one, within 24 hours — never respond in the thread
  • If it involves threats or a child, escalate to your welfare officer and the FA
Most people move between types depending on context. Know the pattern, not just the label.

Mirror neurons mean emotions are literally contagious — we feel what we see others feel.

The Framework

5 Principles of De-escalation

Whether you're managing yourself or someone else on the touchline.
1
Regulate Yourself First
You cannot de-escalate someone else while you are escalated. Check your own rung before you approach.
2
Acknowledge Before Redirecting
People who feel dismissed escalate. People who feel heard calm down. Acknowledgement is not agreement.
3
Calibrate Your Voice & Body
Slow, low, and still. Speak 20% slower. Drop your pitch. Hands visible and low. Turn slightly sideways.
4
Use Distance, Not Authority
Moving someone 20 metres away breaks the stimulus loop and removes the audience that sustains the behaviour.
5
Know When to Stop
Past rung 4, continued engagement often escalates. Your job is not to win — it's to protect the environment.
In Practice

De-escalation In Practice

The principles are only useful if you know what they look like in the moment.
Principle 3 — Voice & Body
WRONG
  • Matching their volume
  • Pointing or stepping toward them
  • Arms crossed, squared on
  • Same pace and pitch as normal
RIGHT
  • 20% slower speech, lower pitch
  • Palms visible, hands below the waist
  • Turn 45° sideways — less confrontational
  • Keep 2 metres minimum distance
Your body language either escalates or de-escalates before you've said a single word.
Principle 2 — Acknowledge First
SAY THIS INSTEAD
"Calm down."
"You're overreacting."
"The referee is just doing his job."
SAY THIS
"I can see you're really frustrated."
"That call was hard to take."
"I get it — that looked wrong from here. Let's stay focused on the team."
Acknowledgement is not agreement. You're not conceding the point — you're lowering the temperature.
5s
The 5-second rule: Never respond to escalating behaviour within the first 5 seconds. Your physiological state won't let you respond well. Pause. Breathe once. Then approach.
After the Incident

The Reflection & Recovery Framework

Managing the moment is crisis management. Reflection is what changes culture.
The 24-hour review — ask these 5 questions
1
What actually happened?
Facts only — no story, no interpretation. Who, what, where.
2
What was my role in how it escalated or de-escalated?
Honest self-assessment. Not blame — learning.
3
What did I do well?
Anchor what worked so you can repeat it.
4
What would I do differently?
One specific change for next time. Keep it concrete.
5
Does this need to be formally logged?
See the reporting thresholds on the right.
Always report formally if:
  • Any physical contact occurred
  • Direct threats were made to a person
  • A child was present and distressed
  • This is a repeated pattern from the same person
  • A referee or official was targeted
Log via your club welfare officer or directly through FA Whole Game System.
Clubs that conduct structured post-incident reviews see significantly fewer repeat incidents. Reflection isn't paperwork — it's the mechanism that turns one bad matchday into lasting culture change.
Say This, Not That

Phrases That Work

Parent/Carer/Guardian shouting at referee
"I need you to step back with me for a second — let's go." [Walk away as you say it — they usually follow]
Player argues with opponent
"Leave it. Not today. I've got you." [Short, calm, no debate. Walk them away]
Your own anger rising
"I'm going to give myself 10 seconds before I respond to that." [Say it aloud if needed]
Crowd getting hostile
"Folks — I need everyone on this side to take it down a level. We're better than this."
Reflective conversation after
"That was a tough one. What was going through your head in that moment?" [Curiosity, not judgment]
Starting points only — adapt to your voice and the specific context.
Action

Three Moves That Matter

01
Share the Escalation Ladder
Send slide 6 to every manager and parent/carer/guardian group. Ask one question: "Which rung do you sometimes find yourself on?" Starting the conversation is the intervention.
Low effort
High impact
02
Assign a Positive-Voice Role
At each match, designate one parent/carer/guardian as Atmosphere Coordinator. Rotate it. When people have a role, they rise to it.
Low effort
High impact
03
Create a 30-Second Team Reset Ritual
End every pre-match huddle with one line about how you play — not the result. Repeated consistently, it sets a cultural norm before the ball is kicked.
Medium effort
Culture-changing
