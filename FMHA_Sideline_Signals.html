Sideline Signals

A matchday reference for coaches and welfare officers. Know what to look for, when to step in, and exactly what to say at each of the five escalation levels before a situation becomes an incident.

Before the match
📋
Brief a designated person Assign one adult per match specifically to monitor the sideline. Not the coach — someone with bandwidth to watch the crowd, not the game.
🧘
Teach breathing before it's needed Breathing techniques only work in a crisis if people have practiced them first. Use training sessions to run them. Box breathing takes 90 seconds to learn.
📍
Position spectators deliberately Separate the two sets of supporters if possible. Proximity fuels escalation. Distance gives everyone room to stay calm.
🤝
Set expectations early A brief, friendly word to parents before kick-off — "just a reminder, positive support only today" — resets the tone before anything has gone wrong.
The escalation ladder — tap each level to expand
Level 1 · Frustrated
Early warning signs
Low-level tension. No intervention needed yet — but watch.
▼
Signs to watch for
Sighing, eye-rolling, visible exasperation at decisions
Quiet complaints to the person standing next to them
Nervous or restless energy — shifting weight, can't stand still
Fists clenching and unclenching quietly
Avoiding eye contact with other spectators or the CWO
Low-level moaning at referee decisions — audible but not aggressive
At this stage
"
No script needed yet. Stay visible. Make eye contact and smile — a calm, friendly presence can settle tension before it surfaces. You don't need to say anything.
Don't
✕Ignore it — things that are noticed early rarely escalate to level 4 or 5
✕Over-react — a formal warning at level 1 can itself create a confrontation
Level 2 · Agitated
It's building
Visible tension. This is your best window — act before it turns verbal.
Best window
▼
This is your best window Intervention is most effective at levels 1–2. The person is still reachable — their rational brain is still in the game. Wait until level 3 and you're dealing with a very different situation.
Signs to watch for
Pacing along the touchline — unable or unwilling to stay in one place
Muttering louder, exaggerated groans at decisions
Tense body language — arms crossed, jaw tight, stiff posture
Constant, controlling direction-giving to players from the touchline
Dismissing or brushing off attempts by others to calm them down
Tension starting to spread to those standing nearby
What to say — word for word
"
Hi — are you OK?" (Simple, non-accusatory. Asking if they're OK breaks the pattern — it shifts them from reactive to reflective.)
"
I can see you're frustrated — totally understandable. Let's have a proper chat about it after the game. Right now the kids need to enjoy this and the ref needs to be able to do their job."
"
Come and stand with me over here for a minute." (Moving them physically — even a few metres — changes the dynamic entirely.)
Don't
✕Start with "you need to calm down" — it almost always makes things worse
✕Challenge them publicly in front of a crowd
✕Argue about whether they're right about the referee's decision
✕Walk away once you've approached — stay with them
Level 3 · Verbal
Verbal abuse
Shouting, swearing, or insulting. Intervention required now.
▼
Signs — intervention is now required
Audible shouting — louder than communication, with a clear edge of aggression
Swearing — either directed or as a general release
Personal insults directed at players, officials, or other spectators
Discriminatory language or gestures of any kind
Shouting instructions or criticism directly at players or the referee
What to say
"
I can see you're really frustrated — and I get it. But I need you to stop. The kids are watching and the language isn't OK." (Acknowledge first — then set the boundary. Don't lead with the rule.)
"
That call was hard to take — I know. Let's stay focused on the team. Come on." (Then move them. Walking away as you say it invites them to follow.)
Don't
✕Argue about whether the referee's decision was correct
✕Allow discriminatory language to go unchallenged, even once
✕Walk away once you've approached — stay with them until they've settled
✕Challenge them publicly with other spectators watching — move them first
Level 4 · Threatening
Threats & intimidation
Direct threats or physical intimidation. Do not approach alone.
▼
Physical risk is now real By level 4, continued one-to-one engagement often escalates rather than de-escalates. Get a second person with you before you approach. Inform the referee immediately.
Signs to watch for
Direct verbal threats to officials, coaches, or other spectators
Squaring up — moving into someone's personal space in a confrontational way
Advancing toward or pursuing the referee or match officials
Encroaching on or moving toward the field of play
Posturing for a fight — weight forward, arms out, voice dropping to a menacing low
What to say — clear and brief
"
I'm going to need you to step away from the touchline now. This isn't a discussion." (Calm, direct, no negotiation. Don't explain or justify — it gives them something to argue against.)
"
The game will be stopped if this continues." (State the consequence. Then inform the referee immediately — they have the authority to suspend or abandon the match.)
Immediate actions
Get a second adult with you before approaching — do not go alone
Inform the referee — they can suspend the game immediately
Begin moving children away from the area while others manage the individual
Start documenting — notes, voice memo, photos of the scene if safe to do so
Don't
✕Approach alone — at level 4 you need a second person present
✕Try to reason or negotiate — it almost always escalates at this stage
✕Allow the game to continue without informing the referee
Level 5 · Physical
Physical incident
Assault, throwing objects, uncontrollable rage. Child safety is the only priority.
Call 999
▼
Signs — this is now a formal incident
Physical assault — pushing, grabbing, striking another person
Throwing objects onto or near the field of play
Uncontrollable rage — the person has lost the ability to regulate themselves
Pursuing or attempting to physically confront the referee
Any situation where a child is at immediate risk of harm
What to do
"
Do not engage physically unless a child's immediate safety demands it. Your first job is to get children away from the situation. Call 999 if there is active violence or a child is in danger.
Immediate actions — in order
Get children physically away from the incident — this takes priority over everything else
Inform the referee — the match must be stopped immediately
Call 999 if there is active violence, a weapon, or a child is at risk
Do not physically intervene yourself unless absolutely necessary to protect a child
Document everything immediately — notes, photos if safe, witness names
Notify your club welfare officer before you leave the ground
Don't
✕Attempt to physically restrain the person unless a child is in immediate danger
✕Allow the match to continue — it must be stopped
✕Wait until after the match to report — this must be logged now while details are fresh
✕Handle this alone — involve your club committee and welfare officer immediately
After the match — if something happened

What to do before you leave the ground

1
Write it down — now Time, location, what was said, who witnessed it. Memory degrades fast under stress. Even a voice note is better than waiting until you get home.
2
Speak to the referee and ask for their report Referee reports carry significant weight in FA disciplinary proceedings. They need to know you're taking it seriously so they file formally.
3
Notify your club committee Under FA Rules E20 and E21, the club — not just the individual — is responsible. The committee needs to know immediately. Don't manage this alone.
4
Check in on the children Young players who witnessed or were near a serious incident may need a quiet word. Ask how they're feeling — don't assume they're fine because the game ended.
5
Follow up in writing with the individual If the person is a club member, a written communication — not a pitch-side conversation — sets the right tone for what happens next. Keep it factual, not emotional.
